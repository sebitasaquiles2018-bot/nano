# Script — 14 inner pages + cover

Each inner page is a single tall composition (3:4 portrait), oil-painting style. Captions in italic narrator voice; dialogue rare and load-bearing.

---

## COVER
**Image:** Sebastian bound to the wooden post, head turned slightly toward the viewer, three arrows in his side and chest. Late golden light, sky behind him going to red and bruised purple. He is not screaming — his face is calm, almost asleep. Title across the top.

**Title:** SAINT SEBASTIAN
**Subtitle:** THE OFFICER WHO WALKED BACK
**Tagline (small):** Rome, c.288 AD

---

## PAGE 1 — Rome, c.286 AD
**Image:** Wide aerial-style oil painting of Rome at dawn — Colosseum, the seven hills, smoke from cooking fires. In the corner, a small Praetorian patrol in red cloaks crossing a marble forum. Looks like a Renaissance vista with a 19th-century academic eye.

**Caption (top):** *Rome, the year 286 of the city's empire. A million souls live behind these walls. The Emperor Diocletian rules from a marble palace.*
**Caption (bottom):** *To worship the Christ in this city is a crime. The reward for denouncing your neighbour is paid in gold.*

---

## PAGE 2 — Captain of the Guard
**Image:** Inside the Praetorian camp at dusk. Sebastian, in muscle cuirass and red cloak, addresses a small file of guardsmen with quiet authority. Helmet under his arm. Lamp-light, brick walls, the eagle standard behind him.

**Caption:** *Sebastian of Narbonne, a captain of the Praetorian Guard. Trusted by the Emperor. Loved by his men.*
**Caption (lower):** *None of them know.*

---

## PAGE 3 — The prisoner's cell
**Image:** A low vaulted cell. A young Christian prisoner kneels in chains, head bowed. Sebastian crouches beside him in the half-dark, one hand on the prisoner's shoulder, helmet set on the straw. He is not in command pose — he is leaning in, listening. A single oil lamp on the floor between them.

**Caption:** *He used his rank to walk into rooms no one else could enter. He brought bread. He brought water. He brought the only thing the chained have ever needed —*
**Caption:** *— someone unafraid to sit beside them.*

---

## PAGE 4 — Chromatius
**Image:** Atrium of a Roman villa. The Prefect of Rome, Chromatius, sits in a heavy carved chair, swollen foot wrapped, face creased with pain — gout. Sebastian stands before him, calm, one hand outstretched. Behind Chromatius, a household shrine still holds the old gods. A single shaft of light comes through the impluvium opening above.

**Caption:** *Chromatius, Prefect of Rome, lay crippled by gout. The doctors had failed. Sebastian asked for one thing —*
**Dialogue (Sebastian, small):** *"Take down the idols. And then we will pray."*

---

## PAGE 5 — The household turns
**Image:** Same atrium, days later. The household shrine niche now holds a simple wooden cross instead of statues of household gods. Chromatius stands — *standing* — with his son Tiburtius beside him, both transformed. Servants gather quietly at the edges. Sebastian off to one side, in plain tunic now (no armor), reading from a scroll. Warm honey-coloured light.

**Caption:** *Chromatius rose from his chair. So did his house — his son Tiburtius, the master of the rolls Nicostratus, the jailer Claudius.*
**Caption:** *One captain in the wrong uniform, undoing the empire one room at a time.*

---

## PAGE 6 — The denouncement
**Image:** Diocletian's audience hall. The Emperor on his curule throne, hand gripping the armrest, face like stone. A kneeling informer — back to us — whispers up at him. A scroll lies between them. Light is cold, from above. Lictors in the shadows behind the throne.

**Caption:** *In a city that pays gold for whispers, no secret keeps forever.*
**Caption (Diocletian, italic, internal):** *Sebastian. My own captain.*

---

## PAGE 7 — Brought before the Emperor
**Image:** Same hall, wide. Sebastian stands alone in the center of the polished floor, helmet under his arm, in full Praetorian dress. Diocletian leans forward on the throne, jabbing a finger toward him in fury. Two guards flank Sebastian — men he commanded yesterday.

**Dialogue (Diocletian, large):** *"I lifted you from nothing. And you give your loyalty to a dead Galilean carpenter?"*
**Dialogue (Sebastian, calm, smaller):** *"I give it to the one who gave the carpenter his hands, Caesar."*

---

## PAGE 8 — The post
**Image:** The archery field outside the camp. Sebastian, stripped to a white loincloth, bound by the wrists above his head to a tall wooden post. Three Mauretanian archers in scale armor and conical helmets, perhaps thirty paces back, drawing composite recurve bows. Long late-afternoon shadows. Distant pines on the horizon. Composition formal, almost ceremonial — like a Renaissance martyrdom panel.

**Caption:** *The Emperor gave the order to archers from Mauretania, who were judged the best in the legions.*
**Caption:** *They did not miss.*

---

## PAGE 9 — Pierced
**Image:** The iconic image, redone with restraint. Tighter framing on Sebastian at the post. Three arrows — one in the right thigh, one in the side, one through the left shoulder. His head is turned upward and slightly to the side, eyes nearly closed, mouth softened. NOT erotic, NOT theatrical — a man enduring. Sky behind has gone to bruised gold and rose. The archers are gone from frame.

**Caption:** *They left him for the crows.*

---

## PAGE 10 — The widow's lamp
**Image:** Night. The same post in pale moonlight. Irene of Rome, in dark stola and headcovering, kneels beside Sebastian, holding a small clay oil lamp close to his face. Her other hand is on his cheek. His eyes are open — barely. A second figure (another widow) holds a folded cloak in the background. The arrows are still in him.

**Caption:** *Irene, a widow of the Christian community, came in the dark to bury him.*
**Caption:** *She found him breathing.*

---

## PAGE 11 — The healing
**Image:** Small upstairs room, oil-lamp light. Sebastian lies on a low pallet, bandaged across the chest and thigh, no longer bound, propped on a folded blanket. Irene seated beside him is grinding something in a small mortar — herbs and oil. The mood is intimate, quiet, restorative. A wooden cross hangs on the wall behind. Dawn light begins through a small high window.

**Caption:** *For weeks he lay in a poor house near the Esquiline. Irene drew the arrows, washed the wounds, fed him a spoonful at a time.*
**Caption:** *He should not have lived. He lived.*

---

## PAGE 12 — The staircase
**Image:** Wide marble palace staircase, cold morning light. Sebastian — healed, in plain tunic and traveler's cloak, no armor — stands at the foot of the staircase, looking up. The staircase rises toward the dark mouth of the imperial wing. He is small in the frame, alone, a single straight figure. The composition is the architecture pressing down on him.

**Caption:** *When his strength returned, he did not flee Rome. He walked to the palace.*
**Caption:** *He waited at the foot of the Emperor's staircase.*

---

## PAGE 13 — Face to face
**Image:** Diocletian, descending the stair flanked by attendants, has stopped two steps above Sebastian. Diocletian's face: shock cracking into something else — fear. Sebastian's face: utterly steady, looking up. He has lifted one hand, palm open, accusing — not violently, but plainly. The attendants have frozen mid-step.

**Dialogue (Diocletian, small, almost whispered):** *"You are dead."*
**Dialogue (Sebastian):** *"Then hear the dead, Caesar. The blood you spill is the blood of innocents. God sees you."*

---

## PAGE 14 — The cudgels
**Image:** Same staircase, moments later. Two Praetorians in red cloaks, faces hidden in shadow under helmets, descend on Sebastian with heavy wooden clubs raised. Sebastian has fallen to one knee at the base of the stair, head bowed, hands open at his sides — not resisting. Composition keeps the violence implied, not explicit; the moment held just before the strike. Cold marble, blood beginning at the corner of his mouth. Diocletian, half-turned away on the staircase above, one hand on the railing.

**Caption:** *The arrows had not killed him. The cudgels did.*
**Caption (smaller):** *They threw his body into the great drain that runs to the Tiber, so that no Christian might find it. A widow named Lucina found it anyway. She buried him in the catacomb on the road to Ostia, where pilgrims still come.*

---

## PAGE 15 — The name in the calendar
**Image:** Typographic page, treated as a manuscript artifact — a fragment of a parchment leaf from the **Chronograph of 354 AD**, in late-Roman uncial calligraphy, slightly aged and lamp-lit. The line for **XIII Kal. Feb. — Sebastiani in Catacumbas** is highlighted by a faint gold outline. Around the parchment: a plain dark wood-grain background, a single oil lamp's worth of light. NO speech bubbles, NO captions overlaid on the parchment.

**Caption (below the parchment, separate, small italic):** *Sixty-six years after he died, his name was already written into the calendar of the city of Rome. Whatever else is legend, this much is fact: Rome did not forget him.*
