# Style guide

## Visual idiom
**Late-Renaissance / Baroque oil painting** — chiaroscuro, deep shadow, single dramatic light source. Think Mantegna, Guido Reni, early Caravaggio depictions of St. Sebastian. NOT modern comic ink; not flat cel shading.

## Palette
- **Imperial reds** (Praetorian cloaks, blood, Diocletian's purple-crimson)
- **Gold** (lamp light, armor highlights, embroidery on imperial robes)
- **Burnt sienna / ochre** (Roman walls, terracotta tile, sand of the field)
- **Deep umber / black** (shadow, night scenes — pages 9–10)
- **Pale flesh tones** under dramatic light, cool stone-grey for marble

Avoid: neon, candy colors, modern saturated comic palettes.

## Camera & composition
- Low-angle hero shots for Sebastian when he stands his ground.
- Eye-level human framing for prisoner/conversion scenes — intimate, not heroic.
- High-angle / overhead for the arrow scene to evoke crucifixion-icon composition.
- Diocletian: enthroned wide shots, then suddenly tight close-up at the staircase confrontation.

## Faces
Painted, weighted, expressive — NOT cartooned. Eyes are where the emotion lives. Sebastian's face stays steady throughout — he is calm, even at the post, even on the staircase. Diocletian's face becomes the one that breaks.

## Lettering / narration
- Captions: small panels of italic narrator text in upper-left of pages, sparing.
- Dialogue: minimal, used only at hinge moments. Latin phrases occasionally, with the English meaning embedded naturally in the surrounding caption.
- One typographic page (#15): Sebastian's name in the Chronograph of 354 — treated as a manuscript artifact.

## Aspect ratio
3:4 portrait inner pages, suited to oil-painting framing.
